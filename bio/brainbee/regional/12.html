Chapter 12 Psychiatric Disorders

  • Anxiety Disorders
    • Include: post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), and panic attacks
    • Most common mental disorders experienced by Americans
    • More common in women
    • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) increase serotonin levels → usually deficient in many psychiatric conditions
    • Benzodiazepines (such as diazepam, or Valium) increase levels of inhibitory gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)
      • GABA → “brake pedal”, help decrease neuron activity in areas of brain involved in anxiety
    • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder
      • Symptoms: uncontrollable, recurring thoughts (obsessions), repeated, ritualistic behaviors (compulsions)
    • Basal ganglia → regulate ability to move and think, helps carry out routine behaviors = habits
    • About 70% of people with OCD can be treated with SSRIs
    • Tricyclic antidepressant clomipramine and neuroleptic (tranquilizing) drugs can be used
    • Cognitive behavioral therapy → form of counseling
    • Deep brain stimulation (DBS) → therapeutic approach used for people with OCD
    • Panic Disorder
      • Characterized by sudden, unexpected periods of intense, irrational fear
      • Physiological symptoms include difficulty breathing, increased heart rate, profuse sweating, and dizziness
      • Panic disorders → treated with psychotherapy, medications, or combination of both
        • SSRIs → primary drugs used for panic disorder
        • Benzodiazepines can be used in emergency
      • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder
        • Caused by traumatic event in adulthood or childhood
        • Altered levels of cortisol and norepinephrine
        • Treatment → drugs that block norepinephrine,
          • Blood-pressure medication prazosin
          • Beta-blocker drugs like propranolol
        • Low levels of serotonin in people with PTSD → SSRIs for treatment
        • Neurotransmitter neuropeptide Y → provides some protection against developing PTSD
        • People with PTSD → have smaller hippocampus (learning and memory) and smaller prefrontal cortex (helps control thinking, emotions, and behavior)
        • Brain’s emotional center = amygdala → is overactive in PTSD
      • Major Depression
        • Symptoms persist for at least 2 weeks
          • Include feeling empty or sad, suicidal thoughts, loss of appetite, irritability, inability to sleep
        • Disrupt hypothalamus
        • Monoamine neurotransmitter systems (dopamine and serotonin) → are affected
        • Depressed people have smaller hippocampus and prefrontal cortex
        • Antidepressants raise levels of norepinephrine, serotonin, and dopamine
        • SSRIs → block serotonin reuptake and are used to treat psychiatric disorders
        • Deep brain stimulation (DBS) → relieve depressive episodes that are resistant to medications
      • Bipolar Disorders
        • Also known as manic-depressive illness
        • Intense and rapid mood changes
        • Moods swing between extreme highs and severe lows
          • High, or manic, episodes → insomnia, harmful and risky behaviors, increased energy, racing thoughts, excitement, flood of ideas
          • Low, or depressive, episodes → sadness, depressed mood, hopelessness, suicidal thoughts
        • Medications include anti-epilepsy drugs, lithium and atypical antipsychotics → for manic periods
      • Antidepressants or cognitive behavioral therapy → for depressive periods
      • Schizophrenia
        • Psychiatric disorder which disturbs thinking and emotion
        • People lose touch with reality
        • “Positive” symptoms → hallucinations, delusions, and confused thinking
        • “Negative” symptoms → depression, lack of motivation, inability to experience pleasure
        • Appears between ages 15 and 25
        • Chlorpromazine → first antipsychotic drug
        • Drugs dampen concentration of dopamine → drives schizophrenia’s “positive” symptoms
          • Medications can induce tremors and movement-related side effects mimicking
        • Parkinson’s disease
          • New drugs inhibit serotonergic activity → help with negative symptoms
          • Nicotine → relax rigid nerve-cell shape and alleviate symptoms
