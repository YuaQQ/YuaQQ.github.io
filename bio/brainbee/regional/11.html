Chapter 11 Childhood Disorders

  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
    • Symptoms: impaired social communication, repetitive behaviors, obsessive interests
  • 4 to 5 times more common in boys
  • Genes include:
    • FMR1 (codes for fragile X mental retardation protein)
    • PTEN (codes for a tumor suppressor enzyme that controls cell proliferation)
    • TSC1 or TSC2 (tuberous sclerosis complex 1 and 2)
    • 50 to 60% of people with fragile X syndrome and approximately 40% of people with tuberous sclerosis have ASD
    • Children with mutation in gene NF-1 develop tumors in childhood (neurofibromatosis) and 10% have autism
  • ASD-related genes influence cellular pathways for regulating cell metabolism, growth, and proliferation → mTOR pathway
  • Can be diagnosed based on behavioral characteristics at age 2
  • Treatment with oxytocin → hormone that improves social bonding
  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
    • Characterized by inattentiveness, hyperactivity, impulsive behaviors
    • Affect dopamine → involved in rewarding behaviors
    • Medications include stimulants such as methylphenidate
  • Down Syndrome
    • Due to 1 extra copy of 21st chromosome (trisomy)
    • Maternal age → major risk factor for Down syndrome
      • Mothers older than 40 are 5 times more likely to have an affected child
    • Diagnosed using amniocentesis and chorionic villus sampling
    • Virtually everyone born with Down will develop early-onset Alzheimer’s disease
    • Chromosome 21 contains gene that codes for amyloid precursor protein (APP)
  • Dyslexia
    • Most common and best-studied learning disability
    • Leads to difficulty with reading
    • Intelligence not affected
    • Symptoms: trouble with pronunciation, lack of fluency, difficulty retrieving words, poor spelling, hesitancy when speaking
    • “Word-form area” affected → involved in recognition of printed letters and words
    • Dyslexia runs in families
  • Epilepsy
    • Diagnosed using EEG
    • Generalized seizures affect both sides of brain
      • Absence or petit mal seizures → rapid blinking or a few seconds of staring into space
      • Tonic-clonic or grand mal seizures → muscle contractions and/or loss of consciousness
    • Focal or partial seizures → localized to one area of brain
    • Secondary generalized seizures → begin in one part of brain and extent to whole brain
    • Causes include premature birth, brain trauma, and abnormal development because of mutations in genes
    • If one side of brain triggers seizures on other side → “split-brain surgery” is performed
      • Severing corpus callosum → band of white matter that connects two hemispheres of brain
