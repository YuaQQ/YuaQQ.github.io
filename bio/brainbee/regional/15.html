Chapter 15 Neurodegenerative Diseases

  • Prions → protein’s normal 3D structure is altered
    • Cause proteins to form clumps that can damage cells
  • Alzheimer’s Disease
    • Form of dementia that is fatal
    • Late-onset form → symptoms in mid-60s or later
    • Early-onset forms → symptoms in 30s
    • Alzheimer’s is most common cause of dementia in older adults
    • Candidate biomarkers → amyloid-beta (also called beta-amyloid) and tau
      • Amyloid-beta forms extracellular plaques, also known as neuritic plaques
        • Formed from malformed clumps of amyloid precursor protein (APP)
      • Tau protein → stabilizes microtubules
        • Forms neurofibrillary tangles inside neurons
        • Crucial to axonal transport
        • Neurofibrillary tangles first seen in entorhinal cortex and hippocampus
      • Early-onset Alzheimer’s → dominantly inherited form of disease
      • Dominant mutations in genes: APP, PSEN1, PSEN2
      • In late-onset Alzheimer’s → ApoE4 is major genetic risk factor but not determining one
      • TOMM40 gene → codes for proteins responsible for moving proteins into mitochondria
      • TREM-2 protein → regulates removal of cell debris, clearing amyloid proteins, and suppressing inflammation in microglia
      • Treatment includes cholinesterase inhibitors: donepezil, galantamine, and rivastigmine
        • Acetylcholinesterase → enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine
        • Acetylcholine → normally involved in cognition, learning and memory
      • Fourth drug, memantine → NMDA receptor antagonist
        • Damaged cells → overwhelmed with calcium → neuronal excitotoxicity
        • Memantine blocks flow of calcium through NMDA channels
      • Parkinson’s Disease
        • Second most common neurodegenerative disorder
        • Symptoms: slow movement, unsteady gait, muscular rigidity, poor coordination and instability, resting tremors
        • Cognitive decline often occurs at later stages
        • Loss of dopamine-producing cells neurons in substantia nigra — midbrain structure that is part of basal ganglia
          • This brain region influences movement, reward, and addiction
        • Early-onset cases → linked to mutations in PARK2 (or PRKN) gene, which codes for protein “parkin”
        • Other genes include alpha-synuclein (SNCA), repeat kinase 2 (LRRK-2), and glucocerebrosidase (GBA)
        • Lewy bodies → accumulations of proteins in neuron bodies
          • Composed of protein alpha-synuclein
          • Alpha-synuclein → involved in dopamine transport in nervous system
        • MPTP (1-methyl-4- phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine) → can help create animal model of Parkinson’s disease
          • MPTP metabolizes into the neurotoxin MPP+ (1-methyl-4-phenylpyridinium) → kills neurons in substantia nigra
        • There are 2 types of stem cells: more flexible fetal tissue and induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cells, which are specialized but reprogrammed adult cells
        • Treatment with levodopa
        • L-Dopa relieves Parkinson’s motor symptoms but does not slow disease progression
        • Dyskinesia → abnormal and uncontrolled involuntary movements
        • Surgical procedure called deep brain stimulation (DBS) → can be used to treat Parkinson’s
      • Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
        • Progressive, fatal motor neuron diseases
        • Also known as Lou Gehrig’s disease
        • Non-Hispanic whites are more likely to develop ALS
        • Motor neurons in spinal cord degenerate and die
          • Voluntary muscles weaken, begin twitching, and atrophy
        • About 25 to 40% of cases → C9ORF72 gene
        • Some individuals with this mutation have both motor neuron and dementia disorders → condition known as ALS- FTD (ALS-frontotemporal dementia)
        • Another 12 to 20% of cases → SOD1
          • Code for superoxide dismutase → enzyme that catalyzes breakdown of superoxide radicals
        • Edaravone and Riluzole → drugs that can help treat ALS
          • Edaravone → antioxidant that inhibits production of free radicals
          • Riluzole → decreases glutamate levels
        • Huntington’s Disease
          • Heritable disease that impairs voluntary movement and cognition
          • HD variant of HTT gene is dominant
          • HTT gene → codes for huntingtin protein, located on chromosome 4
            • Involves abnormal number of repeats of trinucleotide snippet of DNA
              • Cytosine, adenine, and guanine (CAG) normally repeats 10 to 35 times
              • In Huntington’s → 36 to 120 or more times
              • “Expanded” huntingtin protein is susceptible to clumping
            • Brain areas → basal ganglia (voluntary movement) and cortex (cognition, perception, and memory)
            • Approved drug deutetrabenazine → treat chorea associated with Huntington’s disease
