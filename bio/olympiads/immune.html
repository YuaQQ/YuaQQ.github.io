Immune System

Immune System Overview

  • Immunity: ability to avoid biological invasion by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites, toxins).
  • Pathogen: disease-causing organism (e.g., bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi).
  • Toxins: chemicals released by pathogens.
    • Endotoxin: lipopolysaccharide from Gram‑negative bacterial cell walls; stimulates cytokine release.
    • Exotoxin: secreted by living bacteria (e.g., botulinum toxin).

The Lymphatic System

  • Functions: returns excess tissue fluid (lymph) to bloodstream, filters lymph, houses immune cells.
  • Lymphatic vessels: collect interstitial fluid → lymph → returned to blood via subclavian veins.
  • Lymph nodes: filter lymph; contain lymphocytes and macrophages.
  • Primary lymphoid organs:
    • Red bone marrow: site of hematopoiesis; B‑cell maturation.
    • Thymus: T‑cell maturation (shrinks in adulthood).
  • Secondary lymphoid organs:
    • Lymph nodes: activation/proliferation of T and B cells.
    • Spleen: filters blood; red pulp (blood filtration), white pulp (lymphoid tissue).
    • Tonsils, Peyer’s patches: capture pathogens; sites of lymphocyte‑antigen contact.

Immune Cell Development & Circulation

  • Hematopoiesis: stem cells in bone marrow give rise to all blood cells.
  • Lymphocyte recirculation: naïve lymphocytes circulate between blood and lymphoid organs; activated by antigen → differentiate into effector/memory cells.

Innate Immunity (Non‑specific, Rapid)

  • First line (External barriers):
    • Skin: physical barrier, low pH, fatty acids, lysozyme, normal flora.
    • Mucous membranes: sticky mucus traps pathogens; cilia move mucus; acidic environments (stomach, vagina, urine); tears with lysozyme.
  • Second line (Internal defenses):
    • Phagocytes: engulf pathogens.
      • Macrophages: tissue‑resident; present antigens; secrete cytokines.
      • Neutrophils: most abundant phagocyte in blood; short‑lived.
      • Eosinophils: attack parasites; involved in allergies.
      • Basophils/mast cells: release histamine (allergies, inflammation).
    • Dendritic cells: antigen‑presenting cells (APCs); link innate and adaptive immunity.
    • Natural killer (NK) cells: kill virus‑infected/cancer cells via apoptosis; recognize missing/altered MHC‑I.
    • Antimicrobial proteins:
      • Interferons: inhibit viral replication (α, β from infected cells; γ from lymphocytes/NK cells).
      • Complement system: ~30 plasma proteins; activated by microbes or antibody‑antigen complexes.
        • Effects: opsonization, inflammation, membrane attack complex (MAC) → cell lysis.
      • Pyrogens: induce fever (e.g., IL‑1, IL‑6, TNF from leukocytes; act on hypothalamus).
    • Inflammatory response:
      • Triggered by tissue injury/infection.
      • Mast cells release histamine → vasodilation, increased capillary permeability.
      • Extravasation/diapedesis: leukocytes exit capillaries to infection site.
      • Signs: redness, heat, swelling, pain.
    • Pattern recognition:
      • PAMPs (pathogen‑associated molecular patterns): e.g., LPS, peptidoglycan.
      • DAMPs (damage‑associated molecular patterns): from necrotic cells.
      • Receptors: toll‑like receptors (TLRs), NOD‑like receptors.

Adaptive Immunity (Specific, Has Memory)

  • Four key features:
    1. Specificity: recognizes specific antigens via lymphocyte receptors.
    2. Self/non‑self discrimination: eliminates self‑reactive lymphocytes (clonal deletion).
    3. Diversity: vast repertoire of receptors via gene rearrangement.
    4. Immunological memory: faster, stronger secondary response.
  • Antigens: foreign molecules that induce immune response.
  • Epitope: specific part of antigen bound by receptor.
  • Lymphocytes:
    • B cells: mature in bone marrow; mediate humoral immunity (antibody production).
    • T cells: mature in thymus; mediate cellular immunity.
      • Helper T cells (T_H, CD4+): activate B cells and cytotoxic T cells; recognize antigen presented on MHC‑II.
      • Cytotoxic T cells (T_C, CD8+): kill infected/cancer cells; recognize antigen presented on MHC‑I.
  • Antigen presentation:
    • MHC‑I: on all nucleated cells; presents endogenous antigens (e.g., viral proteins) to CD8+ T cells.
    • MHC‑II: on antigen‑presenting cells (macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells); presents exogenous antigens to CD4+ T cells.
  • Clonal selection: antigen binding selects specific lymphocyte → proliferation → clone of effector/memory cells.
  • Primary vs. secondary response:
    • Primary: first exposure; lag period; produces effector and memory cells.
    • Secondary: re‑exposure; faster, stronger due to memory cells.
  • Vaccination: induces primary response without disease, establishing memory.

Humoral Immunity (B Cells & Antibodies)

  • B cell activation:
    • Antigen binds APC cell receptor → internalized and presented on MHC‑II → recognized by T_H cell → cytokines stimulate B cell proliferation/differentiation.
      • Effector B: plasma cell (rich in RER → secrete antibodies)
      • Memory B
  • Antibody (immunoglobulin) structure: Y‑shaped; two heavy chains, two light chains; variable regions bind antigen.
  • Antibody classes:
    • IgG: most abundant in blood; crosses placenta; opsonization, activates complement, neutralization.
    • IgA: found in secretions (saliva, milk, mucus); mucosal defense.
    • IgM: first antibody produced; pentamer; activates complement; high agglutination capacity.
    • IgD: B cell receptor; role in maturation/activation.
    • IgE: allergies; binds mast cells/basophils; defense against parasites.
  • Antibody functions: neutralization, opsonization, agglutination, complement activation.

Cellular Immunity (T Cells)

  • Effector / Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+):
    • Recognize antigen presented on MHC‑I.
    • Release perforin (forms pores) and granzymes (induce apoptosis) in target cells.
  • Helper T cells (CD4+):
    • Recognize antigen presented on MHC‑II.
    • Secrete cytokines (e.g., IL‑2) to activate B cells, cytotoxic T cells, macrophages.

Passive vs. Active Immunity

  • Active immunity: individual produces own antibodies after exposure (infection or vaccination).
  • Passive immunity: transfer of pre‑formed antibodies (e.g., maternal IgG via placenta; antiserum injection).

Immune Disorders

  • Allergies: hypersensitivity to harmless allergens; IgE‑mediated mast cell degranulation → histamine release → inflammation.
    • Anaphylaxis: severe, systemic allergic reaction.
  • Autoimmune diseases: immune system attacks self‑tissues.
    • Examples: rheumatoid arthritis, type I diabetes, multiple sclerosis, lupus.
  • Immunodeficiency: weakened immune response.
    • Primary: genetic (e.g., SCID).
    • Secondary: acquired (e.g., AIDS from HIV).
  • HIV/AIDS:
    • HIV retrovirus infects CD4+ cells (T_H cells, macrophages, dendritic cells).
    • Uses reverse transcriptase to integrate viral DNA into host genome.
    • Progressive loss of T_H cells → immunosuppression → opportunistic infections.
