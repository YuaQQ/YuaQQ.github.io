# Circulatory & Respiratory Systems

# Circulatory System Basics

- **Three Basic Components**:
    - Circulatory fluid (blood/hemolymph).
    - Interconnecting vessels.
    - A muscular heart.

### Types of Circulatory Systems

- **Open Circulatory System**:
    - Circulatory fluid = **hemolymph** (same as interstitial fluid).
    - Hemolymph bathes organs directly in sinuses (spaces).
    - Found in many invertebrates (e.g., insects, mollusks).
- **Closed Circulatory System**:
    - Circulatory fluid = **blood**, confined to vessels.
    - Blood is separate from interstitial fluid.
    - Found in vertebrates and some invertebrates (e.g., annelids).

### Circulatory Patterns

- **Single Circulation**:
    - Blood passes through the heart once per complete circuit.
    - Example: Fish (heart → gills → body → heart).
- **Double Circulation**:
    - Two separate circuits: **Pulmonary** (to lungs) and **Systemic** (to body).
    - Found in amphibians, reptiles, mammals.
    - Oxygen-rich and oxygen-poor blood are kept largely separate.

## Blood Vessels

### Types & Structure

- **Arteries**:
    - Carry blood **away** from the heart (usually oxygenated; exception: pulmonary artery).
    - Thick, elastic, muscular walls to withstand high pressure.
    - Narrow lumen.
    - No valves (except pulmonary artery).
- **Veins**:
    - Carry blood **toward** the heart (usually deoxygenated; exception: pulmonary vein).
    - Thinner walls with less muscle/elastic tissue.
    - Large lumen.
    - Contain **valves** to prevent backflow (aided by skeletal muscle contractions).
- **Capillaries**:
    - Microscopic vessels connecting arterioles and venules.
    - Site of gas/nutrient exchange with tissues.
    - Walls are one endothelial cell thick for efficient diffusion.
    - No valves; very low pressure.

### Vessel Pathway

- Heart → Aorta → Arteries → Arterioles → Capillaries → Venules → Veins → Vena Cava → Heart.

### Blood Pressure & Flow

- **Blood Pressure**: Force exerted by blood against vessel walls.
    - **Systolic Pressure**: Highest pressure during ventricular contraction.
    - **Diastolic Pressure**: Lowest pressure during ventricular relaxation.
- Highest in aorta/arteries; lowest in veins.
- **Velocity**: Slowest in capillaries due to large total cross-sectional area, allowing time for exchange.

### Countercurrent Heat Exchange

- Arteries and veins run adjacent in opposite directions.
- Allows transfer of heat from warm arterial blood to cool venous blood returning from extremities.
- Conserves body heat in cold environments (e.g., legs of birds, flippers of marine mammals).

## The Heart

### Chambers & Structure

- **Atria (left and right)**: Thin-walled upper chambers; receive blood.
- **Ventricles (left and right)**: Thick-walled lower chambers; pump blood out.
    - Left ventricle wall is thicker (pumps blood to entire body).

### Valves

- **Atrioventricular (AV) Valves**:
    - Between atria and ventricles.
    - **Tricuspid** (right side); **Bicuspid/Mitral** (left side).
    - Prevent backflow into atria during ventricular contraction.
- **Semilunar Valves**:
    - Between ventricles and arteries (pulmonary artery, aorta).
    - Prevent backflow into ventricles during relaxation.

### Cardiac Cycle & Sounds

- **"Lub-Dup" Sounds**:
    - **"Lub"**: Closing of AV valves (ventricular systole).
    - **"Dup"**: Closing of semilunar valves (ventricular diastole).
- **Heart Murmur**: Hissing sound due to leaky/defective valve.

### Cardiac Output

- **Cardiac Output (CO) = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)**.
- Typical resting: HR = 72 bpm, SV = 70 mL → CO ≈ 5 L/min (total blood volume).

### Blood Flow Pathway in Humans (Double Circulation)

1. **Systemic Circulation (Body → Heart → Lungs)**:
    - Body (O₂-poor) → Superior/Inferior Vena Cava → Right Atrium → Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Arteries → Lungs.
2. **Pulmonary Circulation (Lungs → Heart → Body)**:
    - Lungs (O₂-rich) → Pulmonary Veins → Left Atrium → Left Ventricle → Aorta → Body.

### Coronary Circulation

- **Coronary Arteries**: Branch from aorta; supply oxygen/nutrients to heart muscle.
- Blockage → **Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)** → heart muscle cell death.

### Heartbeat Regulation

- **Myogenic**: Heartbeat originates within heart muscle.
- **Pacemaker = Sinoatrial (SA) Node**:
    - Located in right atrium.
    - Generates electrical impulses → atria contract.
- **Atrioventricular (AV) Node**:
    - Delays impulse (~0.1 sec) → ensures atria empty before ventricles contract.
- **Bundle of His / Purkinje Fibers**:
    - Conduct impulses to ventricle apex/walls → coordinated contraction.
- **Regulation by Nervous/Endocrine Systems**:
    - **Sympathetic Nerves** (from brain) increase HR.
    - **Parasympathetic Nerves** (vagus nerve) decrease HR.
    - **Hormones** (e.g., adrenaline) increase HR.

### Electrocardiogram (ECG)

- **P Wave**: Atrial depolarization (contraction).
- **QRS Complex**: Ventricular depolarization (contraction).
- **T Wave**: Ventricular repolarization (relaxation).

## Blood Components & Functions

| Component | Primary Function |
| --- | --- |
| **Plasma (55%)** | Liquid matrix; transports nutrients, wastes, hormones, proteins, heat; maintains pH/fluid balance. |
| **Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)** | Transport O₂ (via hemoglobin) from lungs to tissues; transport some CO₂. |
| **White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)** | Immune defense. **Lymphocytes** (B/T cells) produce antibodies; **Phagocytes** ingest pathogens. |
| **Platelets (Thrombocytes)** | Blood clotting; release clotting factors. |

### Blood Clotting Process (Hemostasis)

1. **Vessel Injury** → Platelets adhere to exposed collagen → form temporary plug.
2. **Clotting Factors** (from platelets, damaged cells, plasma) activate **prothrombin → thrombin**.
3. **Thrombin** converts **fibrinogen → fibrin**.
4. **Fibrin** forms mesh → traps RBCs/platelets → stable clot.
5. **Requirements**: Calcium, Vitamin K.

## Gas Exchange & Transport

### Partial Pressure (Pₓ)

- Pressure exerted by a specific gas in a mixture.
- **Pₒ₂** = partial pressure of oxygen.
- At sea level: Atmospheric Pₒ₂ ≈ 160 mm Hg (21% of 760 mm Hg).
- In alveoli: Pₒ₂ ≈ 100 mm Hg (due to humidification, mixing with residual air).

### Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve

- Shows % hemoglobin saturation vs. Pₒ₂.
- **Sigmoidal Shape** due to **cooperative binding**: Binding of first O₂ molecule increases affinity for others.
- **High Pₒ₂ (e.g., lungs)**: High saturation (≈95-100%).
- **Low Pₒ₂ (e.g., exercising tissues)**: O₂ released from hemoglobin.
- **Factors Shifting Curve**:
    - **Right Shift** (decreased affinity; O₂ released more easily): ↑ CO₂, ↑ temperature, ↓ pH (Bohr effect), ↑ 2,3-BPG.
    - **Left Shift** (increased affinity; O₂ held more tightly): ↓ CO₂, ↓ temperature, ↑ pH, fetal hemoglobin.

### Special Oxygen-Binding Proteins

- **Fetal Hemoglobin**:
    - Higher affinity for O₂ than adult hemoglobin.
    - Allows O₂ transfer from maternal blood in placenta.
- **Myoglobin**:
    - In muscle cells; single heme group.
    - Very high affinity for O₂ at low Pₒ₂; acts as O₂ reserve during strenuous exercise.

## Respiratory Systems

### General Principles

- Respiratory surfaces must be **moist, thin, and large** for efficient diffusion.
- Types: Skin (amphibians), gills (fish), tracheae (insects), lungs (mammals).

### Fish Gills & Countercurrent Exchange

- Water flows over gill filaments/lamellae opposite to blood flow.
- Maintains a **partial pressure gradient** along entire capillary → maximizes O₂ uptake.

### Mammalian Respiratory System

### Structures

- **Nostrils → Nasal Cavity → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli**.
- **Alveoli**: Site of gas exchange; surrounded by capillaries.
- **Cilia & Mucus**: In trachea/bronchi; trap/remove debris.

### Ventilation (Breathing)

- **Negative Pressure Breathing**: Diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract → thoracic volume increases → pressure decreases → air rushes in (inhalation). Relaxation reverses process (exhalation).
- **Tidal Volume**: Air inhaled/exhaled per breath.
- **Vital Capacity**: Maximum air volume that can be exhaled after maximum inhalation.
- **Residual Volume**: Air remaining in lungs after forced exhalation.

### Gas Transport

- **O₂ Transport**: Mostly bound to hemoglobin in RBCs.
- **CO₂ Transport**: As bicarbonate ions (70%) in plasma, bound to hemoglobin (20%), dissolved (10%).

### Respiratory Disorders & Adaptations

- **Asthma**:
    - Allergic inflammation → bronchoconstriction, mucus production → narrowed airways → wheezing, shortness of breath.
    - Treated with bronchodilators (quick relief) and anti-inflammatory steroids (long-term control).
- **High Altitude (Hypoxia)**:
    - Lower Pₒ₂ → reduced O₂ saturation → symptoms: headache, nausea, fatigue.
    - **Acclimatization**: Increased ventilation, more RBC production, kidney excretion of bicarbonate to correct pH.
- **Emphysema** (implied): Alveolar damage reduces surface area for gas exchange.
