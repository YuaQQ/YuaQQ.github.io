# Populations

## Definition

- **Population**: Group of individuals of a single species living in the same general area.

## Determining Population Size: Mark-Recapture Method

- Used to estimate wildlife population size.
- **Procedure**:
    1. Capture a random sample, mark/tag each individual, release.
    2. Later, recapture a second sample, count marked individuals.
- **Assumption**: Proportion of marked individuals in recapture equals proportion in entire population.
- **Formula**:
    
    !1768210058052.png
    
    - N = total population size.
    - s = number marked in first capture.
    - n = total number in second capture.
    - x = number marked in second capture.

## Population Characteristics

- **Density**: Number of individuals per unit area/volume.
- **Dispersion**: Pattern of spacing among individuals.
    - **Clumped**: Individuals aggregate in patches (influenced by resources/behavior).
    - **Uniform**: Individuals evenly distributed (influenced by social interactions like territoriality).
    - **Random**: Positions independent (occurs without strong attractions/repulsions).

## Population Growth Models

- **Basic Change**:
    
    !1768210143943.png
    
    - N_{t+1}: population at time t+1.
    - N_t: population at time t.
    - B: births between t and t+1.
    - D: deaths between t and t+1.
- **Per Capita Rates**:
    - **Per capita birth rate (b)**: Average offspring per individual.
    - **Per capita death rate (d)**: Average chance of dying.
    - **Per capita growth rate (r = b - d)**: Individual’s contribution to population growth.
    - If r > 0 (b > d): population grows.
    - If r < 0 (b < d): population shrinks.
    - If r = 0 (b = d): population stable.
- **Exponential Growth**:
    - **Equation**:
    
    !image.png
    
    - **Pattern**: J-shaped curve; occurs in ideal, unlimited environments or during rebound.
    - **Example**: Elephant population growth in Kruger National Park.
- **Logistic Growth**:
    - **Carrying Capacity (K)**: Maximum population size environment can sustain.
    - **Equation**:
    
    !1768210338811.png
    
    - **Pattern**: S-shaped curve; growth slows as N approaches K.
    - **Interpretation**:
        - When N small relative to K: (K-N)/K ≈ 1 → growth near exponential.
        - When N large: (K-N)/K ≈ 0 → growth slows/stops.

## Demography & Life History

- **Demography**: Study of processes affecting birth, death, and growth rates.
- **Life History**: Shows age-based transitions, survivorship, fecundity.
- **Reproductive Strategies**:
    - **Semelparity (Big-Bang)**: Reproduce once then die (favored in variable/unpredictable environments).
        - *Example*: Salmon spawning.
    - **Iteroparity (Repeated)**: Reproduce multiple times (favored in dependable environments).
        - *Example*: Loggerhead turtles.
    - **Parthenogenesis**: Asexual reproduction; females produce offspring from unfertilized eggs.
        - *Examples*: Rotifers, aphids, some bees, some lizards/fishes.

## Survivorship Curves

- Plot of proportion/number surviving at each age.
- **Types**:
    - **Type I**: Low death rate early/mid-life; increases in old age (e.g., humans).
    - **Type II**: Constant death rate across lifespan (e.g., some birds).
    - **Type III**: High death rate early; lower for survivors (e.g., many plants, marine invertebrates).

## Resource Allocation & Life History Trade-offs

- **Principle of Allocation**: Acquired resource units used for one function at a time (maintenance, growth, defense, reproduction).
- **Trade-offs**: Negative relationships among growth, reproduction, survival.
    - *Example*: High early reproduction → slower growth/smaller adult size.
    - *Example*: High investment in reproduction → reduced adult survivorship.

## K-selection vs. r-selection

- **K-selected (Equilibrium Species)**:
    - Population limited by carrying capacity (K).
    - Density-dependent.
    - Traits: Larger, long-lived, fewer offspring, high parental care.
    - *Examples*: Elephants, whales.
- **r-selected (Opportunist Species)**:
    - Population limited by reproductive rate (r).
    - Density-independent.
    - Traits: Smaller, short-lived, many offspring, low/no parental care.
    - *Examples*: Weeds, insects.

## Limiting Factors

- **Density-Independent Factors**: Unrelated to population density.
    - *Examples*: Earthquakes, storms, fires, floods.
- **Density-Dependent Factors**: Increase with population density.
    - *Examples*: Competition for food, waste buildup, predation, disease.
    - *Example*: Yeast in beer fermentation limited by ethanol tolerance (~13%).

## Population Cycles

- Regular boom-bust cycles due to predation/other interactions.
- *Example*: Snowshoe hare & lynx ~10-year cycle.

## Human Population

- **Growth**: Accelerated due to technological advances (agriculture, medicine).
- **Age Structure**: Relative number of individuals in each age group influences growth.
    - **Rapid Growth**: Pyramid shape (e.g., Afghanistan).
    - **No Growth**: More uniform shape (e.g., Italy).
- **Carrying Capacity Concerns**:
    - Reliance on finite fossil fuels.
    - Climate change & ecosystem degradation from population expansion.
- **Future**: Voluntary reduction via lower per capita birth rates needed if carrying capacity exceeded.
