Plant Structure

Plant Organs and Systems

  • Vegetative Organs: Roots, stems, leaves.
  • Root System: Anchors plant; absorbs water/minerals; stores nutrients.
  • Shoot System: Stems and leaves; photosynthesis.
  • Systems are interdependent.

Roots

  • Functions: Anchorage, absorption, storage.
  • Root Types:
    • Taproot System: One main vertical root (eudicots/gymnosperms).
    • Fibrous Root System: Many thin lateral roots (monocots/seedless vascular plants).
  • Root Adaptations:
    • Prop roots: Support tall plants.
    • Pneumatophores: "Air roots" for oxygen capture.
    • Buttress roots: Support tall trunks.
    • Storage roots: Store food/water.
    • Aerial roots: Epiphytic or strangler figs.

Stems

  • Functions: Support leaves/reproductive structures; transport.
  • Structure: Nodes (leaf attachment), Internodes (segments between nodes).
  • Buds:
    • Axillary bud: Potential lateral shoot.
    • Apical bud: At shoot tip; causes elongation; exhibits apical dominance (inhibits axillary buds).
  • Stem Adaptations:
    • Rhizome: Horizontal underground shoot (e.g., ginger).
    • Bulb: Vertical underground shoot with fleshy leaves (e.g., onion).
    • Corm: Solid, swollen underground stem (e.g., taro).
    • Stolon: Horizontal above-ground shoot (e.g., strawberry runners).
    • Tuber: Enlarged storage tip of rhizome/stolon (e.g., potato).

Leaves

  • Primary photosynthetic organ.
  • Structure: Blade (flattened part) and Petiole (stalk).
  • Leaf Types: Simple, Compound, Doubly compound.
  • Leaf Adaptations:
    • Tendrils: For climbing (leaves or stems).
    • Spines: For defense (e.g., cactus).
    • Storage leaves: Thick, succulent.
    • Reproductive leaves: Produce plantlets (e.g., Kalanchoë).
    • Bracts: Colored leaves surrounding flowers (e.g., poinsettia).

Plant Tissues

  • Dermal Tissue System: Outer protective covering.
    • Epidermis (non-woody plants): Tightly packed cells with cuticle (waxy layer) to prevent water loss.
    • Periderm (woody plants): Replaces epidermis in older stems/roots.
    • Specializations: Root hairs (absorption), Trichomes (shoot hairs; defense, reduce water loss).
  • Vascular Tissue System: Long-distance transport (xylem and phloem).
    • Stele: Collective vascular tissue in root/stem.
    • Vascular cylinder (roots) or vascular bundles (stems/leaves).
  • Ground Tissue System: Everything not dermal or vascular.
    • Parenchyma: Thin primary walls; photosynthesis, storage.
    • Collenchyma: Unevenly thickened primary walls (cellulose/pectin); flexible support in young shoots.
    • Sclerenchyma: Thick lignified secondary walls; rigid support (e.g., fibers, sclereids).
    • Pith (internal to vascular tissue) and Cortex (external to vascular tissue).

Plant Growth

  • Indeterminate Growth: Grow throughout life via meristems.
  • Determinate Growth: Organs stop growing after reaching certain size (e.g., leaves, flowers).
  • Life Cycle Types:
    • Annuals: Complete life cycle in one year.
    • Biennials: Require two growing seasons.
    • Perennials: Live for many years.
  • Meristems: Regions of undifferentiated cells.
    • Apical Meristems: At root/shoot tips; primary growth (length).
    • Lateral Meristems: Vascular cambium & cork cambium; secondary growth (girth).

Primary Growth in Roots

  • Root Tip Zones:
    1. Zone of Cell Division: Apical meristem (protected by root cap).
    2. Zone of Elongation: Cells elongate.
    3. Zone of Maturation/Differentiation: Cells mature into tissues.
  • Root Tissue Organization (from outside to center):
    • Epidermis → Cortex → Endodermis → Pericycle → Vascular Tissue (Xylem/Phloem).
  • Endodermis & Casparian Strip: One-cell-thick innermost cortex layer; Casparian strip (waxy belt) blocks apoplastic mineral transfer, forcing selective symplastic entry into stele.
  • Pericycle: Outermost vascular cylinder layer; gives rise to lateral roots.

Primary Growth in Stems

  • Shoot Apical Meristem: Dome of dividing cells; produces leaf primordia.
  • Tissue Organization in Stems:
    • Monocot Stem: Vascular bundles scattered in ground tissue.
    • Eudicot Stem: Vascular bundles arranged in a ring; distinct cortex and pith.

Primary Growth in Leaves

  • Eudicot Leaf Layers:
    • Upper Epidermis: Waxy cuticle; few stomata.
    • Mesophyll:
      • Palisade Mesophyll (upper): Dense, columnar cells for photosynthesis.
      • Spongy Mesophyll (lower): Loosely packed; air spaces for gas exchange.
    • Lower Epidermis: Waxy cuticle; many stomata flanked by guard cells.
    • Veins (Vascular Bundles): Xylem (top), phloem (bottom); enclosed by bundle sheath cells.

Secondary Growth (Woody Plants)

  • Caused by Lateral Meristems:
    • Vascular Cambium: Produces secondary xylem (wood) inward and secondary phloem outward.
    • Cork Cambium: Produces cork (part of periderm) outward, replacing epidermis.
  • Secondary Xylem (Wood):
    • Annual Rings: Formed each growing season.
      • Early (Spring) Wood: Thin-walled, large-diameter cells for water delivery.
      • Late (Summer) Wood: Thick-walled cells for support.
    • Heartwood: Older, non-conducting inner wood.
    • Sapwood: Younger, conducting outer wood.
  • Bark: All tissues external to vascular cambium (secondary phloem + periderm).
