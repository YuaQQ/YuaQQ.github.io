Digestive System & Animal Nutrition

Nutrition & Animal Diets

  • Nutrition: Process of taking in, breaking down, and absorbing food.
  • Types of Animals:
    • Herbivores: Eat mainly plants/algae.
    • Carnivores: Eat other animals.
    • Omnivores: Eat both plants and animals.

Four Stages of Food Processing

  1. Ingestion: Taking food into the mouth.
  2. Digestion: Breaking down food into absorbable molecules.
    • Mechanical Digestion: Physical breakdown (chewing, churning).
    • Chemical Digestion: Enzymatic breakdown (hydrolases).
  3. Absorption: Uptake of nutrients into the bloodstream.
  4. Egestion (Elimination): Removal of undigested waste (feces).
  • Assimilation (additional step): Use of absorbed nutrients by body cells.

Human Digestive System Structure

  • Alimentary Canal (Gastrointestinal Tract): Continuous tube from mouth to anus.
  • Accessory Organs: Secrete digestive substances via ducts.
    • Salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas.

Gland Types

  • Exocrine Glands: Secrete products (enzymes, bile) into ducts leading to external environment (e.g., pancreas acinar cells).
  • Endocrine Glands: Secrete hormones directly into bloodstream (e.g., pancreatic islets of Langerhans).

Organs of Digestion & Their Functions

Mouth (Oral Cavity)

  • Mechanical Digestion: Chewing.
  • Chemical Digestion: Saliva contains salivary amylase (breaks starch into maltose).

Pharynx

  • Junction for food (to esophagus) and air (to trachea).
  • Swallowing Reflex: Epiglottis closes trachea to prevent choking.

Esophagus

  • Muscular tube connecting pharynx to stomach.
  • Peristalsis: Rhythmic muscle contractions that move food bolus.

Stomach

  • Functions:
    • Temporary food storage.
    • Mechanical digestion (churning).
    • Chemical digestion: Gastric juice contains HCl (acid) and pepsin (enzyme digesting proteins).
    • Produces mucus to protect lining from acid.
  • Acid Chyme: Soupy mixture of food and gastric juices.
  • Sphincters:
    • Esophageal (Cardiac) Sphincter: Prevents backflow from stomach.
    • Pyloric Sphincter: Regulates chyme entry into small intestine.
  • Stomach Lining: Replaced every ~3 days due to acid erosion.
  • Conditions:
    • GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease): Chronic acid backflow into esophagus.
    • Stomach Ulcers: Often caused by Helicobacter pylori bacteria infecting mucus layer; produces urease to neutralize acid.

Small Intestine

  • Primary site for chemical digestion and nutrient absorption.
  • Divisions:
    1. Duodenum: Receives digestive secretions; most digestion occurs here.
    2. Jejunum: Nutrient absorption.
    3. Ileum: Absorption; contains lymphoid tissue for defense.
  • Accessory Organ Contributions:
    • Pancreas: Secretes pancreatic juice (neutralizes acid, contains hydrolases: amylase, proteases, lipase, nucleases).
    • Liver: Produces bile.
    • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile.
  • Bile Function: Emulsifies fats (breaks large globules into droplets) for easier digestion by lipase. Does not chemically digest fat.
  • Absorption Structures:
    • Villi: Finger-like projections increasing surface area.
    • Microvilli: Microscopic projections on epithelial cells ("brush border").
  • Absorption Process:
    • Carbohydrates & Amino Acids: Absorbed into blood capillaries → transported to liver via hepatic portal vein.
    • Lipids: Absorbed as fatty acids/glycerol → form chylomicrons → enter lacteals (lymphatic capillaries) → enter bloodstream.

Lipid Transport in Blood

  • Chylomicrons: Transport dietary triglycerides from intestine.
  • VLDL (Very Low-Density Lipoprotein): Transports endogenous triglycerides from liver.
  • LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein): "Bad cholesterol"; delivers cholesterol to tissues; associated with plaque.
  • HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein): "Good cholesterol"; removes cholesterol from tissues to liver.

Large Intestine (Colon)

  • Functions:
    • Absorbs water and minerals (~90% of secreted water).
    • Compacts undigested material into feces.
  • Feces Composition: Indigestible plant fibers (cellulose), bacteria, bile pigments, dead intestinal cells.
  • Appendix: Small finger-like extension near junction with small intestine; lymphoid function.
  • Rectum: Stores feces.
  • Anus: Elimination opening.
  • Conditions:
    • Diarrhea: Reduced water absorption due to infection/irritation.
    • Constipation: Insufficient fiber or water; sluggish colon movement.

Summary of Digestive Organ Functions

  • Stomach: Storage, protein digestion, kills pathogens.
  • Liver: Produces bile, stores glycogen, metabolizes fats, produces blood proteins, detoxifies blood, stores vitamins.
  • Small Intestine: Digestion, absorption of nutrients.
  • Large Intestine: Absorbs water/minerals, compacts feces.

Chemical Digestion Overview (Enzymes & Sites)

Carbohydrate Digestion

  • Mouth: Salivary amylase (starch → smaller polysaccharides, maltose).
  • Small Intestine: Pancreatic amylase (polysaccharides → disaccharides).
  • Brush Border Enzymes: Maltase, sucrase, lactase (disaccharides → monosaccharides).

Protein Digestion

  • Stomach: Pepsin (proteins → polypeptides).
  • Small Intestine: Trypsin, chymotrypsin (polypeptides → smaller peptides); carboxypeptidase, aminopeptidase, dipeptidases (peptides → amino acids).

Lipid Digestion

  • Small Intestine: Bile emulsification → pancreatic lipase (triglycerides → fatty acids + monoglycerides).

Nucleic Acid Digestion

  • Small Intestine: Pancreatic nucleases (DNA/RNA → nucleotides); brush border enzymes (nucleotides → nitrogenous bases, sugars, phosphates).

Nutritional Requirements

Three Primary Needs

  1. Chemical Energy: From carbohydrates, proteins, lipids (cellular respiration & storage).
  2. Organic Molecules for Synthesis: Carbon (e.g., sugars) and nitrogen (e.g., amino acids) for growth/maintenance.
  3. Essential Nutrients: Substances the body cannot synthesize.

Essential Nutrients (Four Classes)

  1. Essential Fatty Acids:
    • Unsaturated fatty acids (e.g., linoleic acid) that must be obtained from diet (seeds, grains, vegetables).
    • Used for phospholipids, signaling molecules (prostaglandins).
  2. Essential Amino Acids:
    • 9 amino acids (e.g., lysine, methionine) must come from food.
    • Complete Proteins: Contain all essential amino acids (meat, eggs, dairy).
    • Incomplete Proteins: Lack one or more (most plant proteins).
  3. Vitamins:
    • Organic molecules required in small amounts; often act as coenzymes.
    • Water-Soluble: B vitamins (B1-B12) and Vitamin C.
    • Fat-Soluble: Vitamins A, D, E, K.
  4. Minerals:
    • Inorganic nutrients; required in small amounts.
    • Functions: Enzyme cofactors, bone structure (Ca, P), oxygen transport (Fe in hemoglobin), nerve/muscle function (Na, K, Cl), thyroid hormones (I).

Vitamin & Mineral Deficiency Diseases

  • B1 (Thiamin): Beriberi.
  • B3 (Niacin): Pellagra.
  • Vitamin C: Scurvy.
  • Vitamin D: Rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults).
  • Iron: Anemia.
  • Iodine: Goiter.

Dietary Deficiencies

  • Undernourishment: Chronic insufficient caloric intake → body uses stored fats/carbs, then breaks down proteins → muscle loss, organ damage.
  • Malnourishment: Long-term lack of one or more essential nutrients → deformities, disease, death; correctable by dietary change.
