Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation Overview

  • Osmoregulation: regulation of solute concentrations and water balance to maintain internal fluids within narrow limits.
  • Osmotic challenges: animals must balance water and solute gain/loss relative to their environment.

Osmoregulatory Strategies

  • Stenohaline: cannot tolerate large changes in external osmolarity (most animals).
  • Euryhaline: can survive large fluctuations in external osmolarity.
  • Osmoconformers: are iso-osmotic with surroundings; do not regulate osmolarity (e.g., most marine invertebrates).
  • Osmoregulators: expend energy to maintain osmotic balance (e.g., most vertebrates, some invertebrates).

Nitrogenous Waste Excretion

  • Ammonia:
    • Very soluble, highly toxic.
    • Requires large amounts of water for excretion.
    • Seen in aquatic animals (e.g., bony fishes).
  • Urea:
    • Less toxic, soluble.
    • Requires moderate energy to produce.
    • Seen in mammals, most amphibians, sharks.
  • Uric acid:
    • Insoluble, low toxicity.
    • Requires most energy to produce but minimal water.
    • Seen in insects, land snails, reptiles, birds.

Excretory Processes

  • Four key steps: Filtration → Reabsorption → Secretion → Excretion.
  • Produces urine as fluid waste.

Excretory Systems Across Animals

  • Protonephridia (e.g., flatworms):
    • Flame bulbs with cilia draw fluid into tubules.
    • Excrete dilute urine; function in osmoregulation and waste removal.
  • Metanephridia (e.g., earthworms):
    • Paired structures per segment; collect fluid from coelom.
    • Reabsorb solutes; excrete dilute urine with nitrogenous wastes.
  • Malpighian tubules (e.g., insects):
    • Open into digestive tract; tips immersed in hemolymph.
    • Transport epithelium secretes solutes → water follows by osmosis.
    • Most solutes reabsorbed at rectum; uric acid excreted with feces.
  • Kidneys (vertebrates):
    • Major excretory and osmoregulatory organ.
    • Functional unit = nephron.

Osmoregulation in Fish

  • Freshwater fish:
    • Hyperosmotic to environment → gain water, lose salts.
    • Adaptations: produce large volumes of dilute urine; actively absorb NaCl at gills.
  • Marine fish:
    • Hypoosmotic to environment → lose water, gain salts.
    • Adaptations: drink seawater; excrete concentrated urine; actively secrete NaCl at gills.

Mammalian Kidney Structure

  • Cortex: outer region.
  • Medulla: inner region.
  • Renal pelvis: central cavity drained by ureter.
  • Nephron: functional unit; consists of:
    • Glomerulus: ball of capillaries.
    • Bowman’s capsule: cup-shaped swelling surrounding glomerulus.
    • Renal tubule: proximal convoluted tubule, loop of Henle, distal convoluted tubule, collecting duct.

Kidney Function: Nephron Processes

  • Ultrafiltration (Filtration):
    • Occurs in glomerulus/Bowman’s capsule.
    • High blood pressure (due to afferent arteriole > efferent arteriole diameter) forces plasma through filtration barrier.
    • Filtration barrier: glomerular endothelium (fenestrated) → basement membrane (filters proteins) → podocyte slit membrane.
    • Filtrate contains water, ions, glucose, amino acids, urea; excludes cells and large proteins.
  • Reabsorption:
    • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT):
      • Active transport of glucose, amino acids, Na⁺.
      • Passive reabsorption of Cl⁻, water (osmosis).
      • Microvilli increase surface area.
    • Loop of Henle:
      • Descending limb: permeable to water → water leaves by osmosis (medullary concentration gradient).
      • Ascending limb: impermeable to water; active transport of NaCl out → maintains medullary gradient.
    • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT) & Collecting duct:
      • Fine-tuning of water and salt reabsorption.
      • Permeability regulated by hormones (ADH, aldosterone).
  • Secretion:
    • Active transport of substances (e.g., H⁺, K⁺, drugs) from blood into tubule lumen.
    • Occurs mainly in PCT and DCT.
  • Excretion:
    • Final urine passes to renal pelvis → ureter → bladder → urethra.

Regulation of Water & Solute Balance

  • Antidiuretic hormone (ADH / vasopressin):
    • Released from posterior pituitary when blood osmolarity increases.
    • Increases water permeability of DCT and collecting duct → water reabsorption → concentrated urine.
  • Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS):
    • Triggered by low blood pressure/volume.
    • Renin (from juxtaglomerular apparatus) converts angiotensinogen (liver) → angiotensin I.
    • Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) converts angiotensin I → angiotensin II.
    • Angiotensin II: vasoconstriction (↑ BP), stimulates aldosterone and ADH release.
    • Aldosterone (adrenal cortex): increases Na⁺ reabsorption (and K⁺ excretion) in DCT/collecting duct → water retention → ↑ blood volume/pressure.
  • Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP):
    • Released by heart atria in response to high blood volume/pressure.
    • Inhibits renin release, promotes Na⁺ and water excretion → lowers blood volume/pressure.

Composition Comparisons

  • Blood plasma (renal artery): contains cells, proteins, glucose, ions, urea.
  • Glomerular filtrate: similar to plasma but no large proteins/cells.
  • Urine: no glucose/proteins; high urea concentration; variable water/salt content based on hydration.

Glucose Handling & Diabetes

  • Normally, all filtered glucose is reabsorbed in PCT via active transport.
  • Renal threshold: ~9-10 mmol/L blood glucose.
  • In diabetes mellitus: hyperglycemia → filtered glucose exceeds reabsorption capacity → glucose appears in urine (glycosuria) → osmotic diuresis (polyuria).

Adaptations for Water Conservation

  • Animals in arid environments often have longer loops of Henle to create steeper medullary concentration gradients → produce more concentrated urine.
  • E.g., desert mammals, birds.

Key Hormonal Effects Summary

  • ADH: ↑ water reabsorption (collecting duct permeability).
  • Aldosterone: ↑ Na⁺ reabsorption, ↑ K⁺ excretion.
  • ANP: ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption, ↑ water excretion.
  • Angiotensin II: vasoconstriction, stimulates aldosterone/ADH release.
