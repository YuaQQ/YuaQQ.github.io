Animal Behaviors

Animal Behavior

Definition & Purpose

  • Behavior: Action/response carried out by muscular/hormonal systems under nervous system control, often in response to stimuli.
  • Ethology: Scientific study of animal behavior.
  • Behavior helps an animal:
    • Forage (obtain food).
    • Mate (find partner for sexual reproduction).
    • Maintain homeostasis.

Innate vs. Learned Behavior

Innate Behavior

  • Characteristics:
    • Automatically developed, fixed, "built-in"; no learning curve.
    • Independent from environment (all individuals exhibit it).
    • Controlled by genes → subject to natural selection.
  • Examples: Early survival, reproduction, kinesis, taxis, migration, fixed action patterns.

Learned Behavior

  • Characteristics:
    • Modified by experience.
    • Variable, changeable.
  • Types:
    • Individual Learning: Modified by personal experience; not transmitted to next generation.
    • Cultural Transmission (Social Learning): Copied from others; transmissible across generations.

Specific Innate Behaviors

Kinesis vs. Taxis

  • Taxis: Directional movement toward (positive) or away from (negative) a stimulus.
    • Phototaxis: Movement in response to light.
    • Chemotaxis: Movement in response to chemicals.
  • Kinesis: Non-directional change in rate of movement or activity in response to a stimulus.

Fixed Action Patterns (FAPs)

  • Sequence of unlearned, innate behaviors; unchangeable once initiated.
  • Triggered by a simple external cue (sign stimulus).
  • Example:
    • Male stickleback fish attacks anything with a red underside (sign stimulus).
    • Begging behavior in birds, web spinning by spiders.

Learning & Cognition

Habituation

  • Simple learning involving loss of responsiveness to irrelevant stimuli.
  • Example: Birds stop responding to alarm calls if no actual attack follows.

Imprinting

  • Combines learning + innate components; generally irreversible.
  • Occurs during a sensitive period (time when development occurs most easily).
  • Critical Period: Time when certain events must occur for normal development.
  • Examples:
    • Young geese following their mother (or a moving object).
    • Conservation programs using human "parents" to lead whooping crane migrations.

Spatial Learning

  • Modification of behavior based on experience with spatial environment.
  • Navigation: Moving toward a destination.
  • Orientation: Adopting position/path relative to environmental cue.
  • Mechanisms:
    • Trail Following: Ants use pheromone trails.
    • Sun Compass: Homing pigeons use sun position + circadian clock.
    • Magnetic Field Detection: Homing pigeons, loggerhead sea turtles.
    • Landmark Use: Hills, visual cues.
  • Example: Honey bee waggle dance communicates food location (distance/direction relative to sun).

Associative Learning

  • Learning to associate a stimulus with a consequence.
  • Operant Conditioning (Trial-and-Error):
    • Associate behavior with reward/punishment.
    • Example: Rat learns to press lever for food.
  • Classical Conditioning (Pavlovian):
    • Associate neutral stimulus with significant stimulus.
    • Example: Pavlov's dogs salivate to bell after pairing with food.

Cognition & Problem Solving

  • Cognition: Process involving awareness, reasoning, recollection, judgment.
    • Example: Honeybees distinguish "same" vs. "different."
  • Problem Solving: Devising strategy to overcome obstacle.
    • Examples: Chimpanzees stack boxes to reach food; ravens pull up string to get suspended food.

Social Learning

  • Learning through observation of others; forms roots of culture.
  • Examples:
    • Young chimps learn to crack nuts by watching elders.
    • Vervet monkeys use distinct alarm calls for different predators.

Social Behavior & Altruism

Benefits & Costs of Group Living

  • Benefits: Protection from predators (e.g., flocking birds spot predators earlier).
  • Costs: Increased visibility, disease spread, resource depletion.

Altruism & Inclusive Fitness

  • Altruism: Behavior reducing individual fitness but increasing fitness of others.
  • Inclusive Fitness: Total effect on proliferating genes via own offspring + helping relatives produce offspring.
  • Selfish Gene Theory: Alleles promoting their own propagation are favored.

Hamilton's Rule & Kin Selection

  • Hamilton's Rule: Natural selection favors altruism when rB > C.
    • r = coefficient of relatedness (fraction of shared genes).
    • B = benefit to recipient.
    • C = cost to altruist.
  • Kin Selection: Natural selection favoring altruism toward relatives.
    • Example: Belding's ground squirrels give alarm calls to warn relatives.
    • Example: Naked mole rats help reproductive queen/king (close relatives).

Reciprocal Altruism

  • Altruism toward unrelated individuals if favor is returned later.
  • Requires stable social groups, repeated interactions, punishment of cheaters.
  • Example: Human altruism; Prisoner's Dilemma scenarios.

Mating Behavior

  • Mating Systems:
    • Promiscuous: No strong pair bonds.
    • Monogamous: One male + one female.
    • Polygamous:
      • Polygyny: One male + many females.
      • Polyandry: One female + many males.
  • Sexual Dimorphism: Physical differences between males/females.
  • Mate Choice Copying: Individuals copy mate choices of others.
  • Intrasexual Selection: Male-male competition (e.g., agonistic behavior—ritualized contests for mates/resources).

Foraging Behavior & Optimal Foraging Theory

Evolution of Foraging Behavior

  • Example: Drosophila larvae foraging behavior influenced by gene alleles; natural selection favors different alleles based on population density.

Optimal Foraging Model
