Cell Signaling

Electrical Signaling

  • Speed: Fast, non-persistent.
  • Mechanism: Transmission of ions between cytoplasm of neighboring cells via gap junctions.
  • Example: Cardiac muscle cell contraction.

Chemical Signaling (Reception – Transduction – Response)

  • Speed: Slower, prolonged effect.
  • Ligand Types:
    • Hydrophilic Ligands:
      • Peptide hormones: Insulin, growth factors (FGF, IGF, TGF, EGF).
      • Amine hormones.
      • Adrenal medulla hormones: Epinephrine, norepinephrine.
    • Hydrophobic Ligands:
      • Steroid hormones.
      • Sex hormones: Estradiol, testosterone.
      • Adrenocortical hormones:
        • Glucocorticoids (e.g., cortisol).
        • Mineralocorticoids (e.g., aldosterone).
      • Thyroid hormones: Triiodothyronine (T₃), thyroxine (T₄).
  • Signaling Distance Types:
    • Autocrine: Ligand acts on the cell that produced it.
    • Paracrine: Ligand diffuses to act on nearby cells/tissues.
    • Juxtacrine: Ligand acts on a target in direct physical contact.

2. Conformational change in intracellular domain.

2. Binds cytoplasmic/nuclear receptors to form complex.

GPCR Signaling Pathway

  • Reception: Ligand binds GPCR.
  • Transduction:
    1. GPCR recruits G protein.
    2. G protein exchanges GDP for GTP → activated.
    3. Activated G protein activates effector enzyme (e.g., adenylyl cyclase).
    4. Effector enzyme synthesizes second messenger (e.g., cAMP).
    5. Second messenger activates downstream kinase (e.g., PKA) → phosphorylation cascade.
  • Response: Cellular change (e.g., glycogen breakdown via epinephrine signaling).

Protein Kinase-Associated Receptor Signaling (e.g., Growth Factors)

  • Reception: Ligand binds receptor → receptor dimerization → auto-phosphorylation on tyrosine/serine/threonine residues.
  • Transduction:
    1. Receptor activates Ras protein (GDP → GTP exchange).
    2. Activated Ras triggers phosphorylation cascade (e.g., MEK → MAPK → Jun/Ets).
  • Response: Cell proliferation, apoptosis, differentiation.

Ion Channel Receptor Signaling (e.g., Neurotransmitters)

  • Reception: Ligand binds receptor.
  • Transduction: Conformational change opens ion channel → facilitated diffusion of ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺ influx; K⁺ outflow).
  • Response: Change in membrane potential or cellular activity.

Cellular Fluorescence

Fluorophores

  • Excitation & Emission: Always excited at shorter wavelength, emits at longer wavelength (absorbs more energy than emitted).
  • GFP Structure: Beta-pleated sheet-barrel with central fluorophore in hydrophobic environment.
  • GFP Fluorophore: -Ser65-Tyr66-Gly67- sequence.
    • Tyrosine: Aromatic ring (can be substituted with Phe, Trp, His).
    • Glycine: Allows twisting to form loop → brings Tyr closer.
    • Cyclization via -NH₂ of Tyr & C=O of Ser → oxidation forms fluorophore.
  • Fluorophore Formation: Conjugation in the peptide sequence creates fluorescence.
  • Variants via Amino Acid Substitutions:
    • BFP: -Ser65-His66-Gly67-
    • Brighter GFP: -Thr65-Tyr66-Gly67-
    • CFP: -Ser65-Trp66-Gly67-
  • Red Fluorescence (e.g., DsRed): Extended conjugation system shifts emission to longer wavelengths.
