# Ecosystem

# Ecosystems & Biomes

## Ecosystem Definition

- All organisms in a community plus abiotic factors.
- Includes bacteria categorized by:
    - **Oxygen requirement**: Aerobic / anaerobic.
    - **Temperature preference**:
        - **Thermophile**: Grows at 45–122°C.
        - **Mesophile**: Thrives at 15–45°C.
        - **Cryophile/Psychrophile**: Thrives below 15°C.

## Biome Definition

- **Terrestrial Biomes**: Large areas with similar climate & ecosystems; distribution controlled by climate & disturbance.
- **Aquatic Biomes**: Characterized by physical & chemical environment.

# Terrestrial Biomes

## Climate & Classification

- **Climograph**: Plot of annual mean temperature & precipitation.
- Pattern of climatic variation (e.g., seasonal vs. regular precipitation) is as important as averages.
- **Characterization factors**: Latitude, climate, predominant vegetation, adapted microorganisms/fungi/animals, disturbances (e.g., wildfires, hurricanes).

## Major Terrestrial Biomes

### 1. Tropical Forest

- **Distribution**: Equatorial/subequatorial.
- **Precipitation**:
    - Rainforest: Constant; 200–400 cm/year.
    - Dry Forest: Seasonal; 150–200 cm/year.
- **Temperature**: High year-round (25–29°C avg).
- **Vegetation**:
    - Rainforest: Evergreen broadleaf trees with epiphytes.
    - Dry Forest: Deciduous broadleaf trees.

### 2. Desert

- **Distribution**: Near 30° N/S latitude; continental interiors.
- **Precipitation**: Low & variable (<30 cm/year).
- **Temperature**: Highly variable seasonally & daily (can be >50°C or <-30°C).
- **Vegetation**: Low, scattered succulents, deeply rooted shrubs, herbs.
- **Animals**: Reptiles, birds, rodents, insects.

### 3. Savanna

- **Distribution**: Equatorial/subequatorial.
- **Precipitation**: Seasonal; 30–50 cm/year; dry season lasts 8–9 months.
- **Temperature**: Warm year-round (24–29°C avg); more seasonal variation than tropical forests.
- **Vegetation**: Scattered trees, grasses, forbs.
- **Animals**: Large herbivores, lions, hyenas, insects.

### 4. Chaparral

- **Distribution**: Mid-latitude coastal regions.
- **Precipitation**: 30–50 cm/year; rainy winter, dry summer.
- **Temperature**: Cool fall/winter/spring (10–12°C avg); hot summer (30–40°C).
- **Vegetation**: Dominated by shrubs; grasses & herbs.
- **Animals**: Deer, goats, amphibians, birds, reptiles, insects.

### 5. Temperate Grassland

- **Distribution**: South African veldts, Hungarian puszta, Argentine pampas, Russian steppes, North American plains/prairies.
- **Precipitation**: 30–100 cm/year; dry winter, rainy summer.
- **Temperature**: Cold winter (< -10°C); hot summer (up to 30°C).
- **Vegetation**: Grasses & forbs.
- **Animals**: Bison, wild horses, burrowing animals (e.g., prairie dogs).

### 6. Taiga (Boreal Forest)

- **Distribution**: Broad band across northern North America & Eurasia.
- **Precipitation**: 30–70 cm/year; periodic drought.
- **Temperature**: Cold winter (can be < -50°C); warm summer (>20°C).
- **Vegetation**: Dominated by conifers.
- **Animals**: Migratory birds; moose, tigers, brown bears; insects.

### 7. Temperate Broadleaf Forest

- **Distribution**: Mid-latitudes in Northern Hemisphere; smaller areas in Chile, South Africa, Australia, New Zealand.
- **Precipitation**: 70–200 cm/year; all seasons.
- **Temperature**: Cold winter (0°C avg); hot & humid summer (up to 35°C).
- **Vegetation**: Broadleaf trees (no epiphytes); shrubs, herbs.
- **Animals**: Migratory birds; hibernating animals; insects.

### 8. Tundra

- **Types**:
    - **Arctic Tundra**: Expansive Arctic areas.
    - **Alpine Tundra**: High mountains at all latitudes.
- **Precipitation**:
    - Arctic: 20–60 cm/year.
    - Alpine: May be >100 cm/year.
- **Temperature**: Cold winter (< -30°C); cool summer (<10°C).
- **Vegetation**: Grasses, forbs, mosses, lichens; dwarf shrubs.
- **Animals**: Musk oxen (resident); caribou/reindeer (migratory); migratory birds; bears, wolves, foxes.

# Aquatic Biomes

## 1. Lakes

- **Physical Environment**: Standing water; light decreases with depth → stratification.
    - Temperate lakes: Seasonal thermocline.
    - Tropical lowland lakes: Year-round thermocline.
- **Chemical Environment**:
    - **Oligotrophic**: Nutrient-poor, oxygen-rich.
    - **Eutrophic**: Nutrient-rich, anoxic in deep areas in summer/under ice.
- **Geologic Features**: Oligotrophic lakes have less surface area relative to depth.
- **Photoautotrophs**:
    - Littoral zone: Rooted & floating plants.
    - Limnetic zone: Phytoplankton (e.g., cyanobacteria).
- **Heterotrophs**:
    - Benthic zone: Invertebrates.
    - Limnetic zone: Zooplankton.
    - Fish in all oxygen-sufficient zones.

## 2. Wetlands

- **Physical Environment**: Habitat inundated by water at least sometimes; supports water-adapted plants.
- **Chemical Environment**: Periodically low in oxygen; high nutrient/pollutant filtering capacity.
- **Geologic Features**:
    - **Basin Wetlands**: In shallow basins (upland depressions to filled lakes).
    - **Riverine Wetlands**: Along shallow, flooded river/stream banks.
    - **Fringe Wetlands**: Along coasts of large lakes/seas; includes freshwater & marine biomes.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Pond lilies, cattails, sedges, bald cypress, black spruce.
    - **Bogs**: Dominated by sphagnum moss.
    - **Swamps**: Dominated by woody plants.
- **Heterotrophs**:
    - Herbivores: Crustaceans, aquatic insect larvae, muskrats.
    - Carnivores: Dragonflies, otters, frogs, alligators, herons.

## 3. Streams & Rivers

- **Physical Environment**: Vertical zones; headwaters cold/clear/swift; downstream warm/turbid.
- **Chemical Environment**: Salt & nutrient content increases downstream; headwaters oxygen-rich.
- **Geologic Features**: Headwater channels narrow/rocky; downstream silty.
- **Photoautotrophs**: In headwaters: phytoplankton/rooted aquatic plants.
- **Heterotrophs**: Fish & invertebrates.

## 4. Estuaries

- **Physical Environment**: Seawater (denser) at bottom; river water (less dense) at surface.
- **Chemical Environment**: Variable salinity; nutrient-rich from rivers → high productivity.
- **Geologic Features**: Complex network of tidal channels, islands, levees, mudflats.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Phytoplankton, saltmarsh grasses, algae.
- **Heterotrophs**: Worms, oysters, crabs, fish, waterfowl, some marine mammals.

## 5. Intertidal Zones

- **Physical Environment**: Periodically submerged/exposed by tides (twice daily).
- **Chemical Environment**: High oxygen & nutrients; renewed with tides.
- **Geologic Features**: Rocky or sandy substrates.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Seagrass, algae.
- **Heterotrophs**: Worms, clams, crustaceans, sponges, sea anemones, echinoderms, small fishes.

## 6. Oceanic Pelagic Zone

- **Physical Environment**: Vast open water mixed by currents; high clarity; thermally stratified.
- **Chemical Environment**: High oxygen; lower nutrients than coastal; turnover renews nutrients.
- **Geologic Features**: Covers ~70% Earth; avg depth ~4,000m.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Phytoplankton.
- **Heterotrophs**: Zooplankton (most abundant), marine mammals, fish, sea turtles, squids.

## 7. Coral Reefs

- **Physical Environment**: Photic zone of stable tropical marine areas; high clarity.
- **Chemical Environment**: High oxygen; excluded by high freshwater/nutrient inputs.
- **Geologic Features**: Solid substrate.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Unicellular algae (in coral tissues), red/green algae.
- **Heterotrophs**: Corals, fish, invertebrates.

## 8. Marine Benthic Zone

- **Physical Environment**: Seafloor below neritic & pelagic zones; abyssal areas: low temp, high pressure.
- **Chemical Environment**: Oxygen sufficient except organically enriched areas.
- **Geologic Features**: Soft sediments; rocky substrates on reefs, mountains, new crust.
- **Photoautotrophs**: Algae.
- **Chemoautotrophs**: Prokaryotes oxidizing H₂S at hydrothermal vents.
- **Heterotrophs**: Invertebrates (e.g., giant tube worms, echinoderms, arthropods), fishes.

# Ecosystem Processes & Energy Flow

## Physical Laws in Ecosystems

- **First Law of Thermodynamics**: Energy conserved; enters as solar radiation, transformed.
- **Second Law of Thermodynamics**: Energy conversions increase entropy; some energy lost as heat.
- **Law of Conservation of Mass**: Matter conserved; elements recycled.

## Nutrient Cycling

- **Generalized Cycle**:
    - Consumers & producers incorporate nutrients.
    - Decomposers (detritivores) return nutrients to abiotic reservoir.
    - Abiotic reservoir replenished via geologic processes.
    - Decomposition connects all trophic levels.

## Primary Production

- **Definition**: Amount of light energy converted to chemical energy by autotrophs in a given time.
- **Key Equations**:
    - Gross Primary Production (GPP): Total energy stored during photosynthesis.
    - Net Primary Production (NPP) = GPP – Respiration (R).
    - NPP = energy available to primary consumers & decomposers.
- **Limiting Factors**:
    - **Aquatic**: Light, nutrients (N, Fe, P).
    - **Terrestrial**: Temperature, precipitation, mineral nutrients.
- **Eutrophication**: Excessive algal growth from nutrient runoff → blocks light → plant death → decomposition depletes O₂ → anoxic conditions → organism death.

## Energy Transfer & Trophic Efficiency

- **Trophic Efficiency**: % of production transferred between trophic levels (~10%; range 5–20%).
- **Ecological Pyramid**: Illustrates energy loss between levels.
    - Primary producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → tertiary consumers.
    - Energy decreases by ~90% at each step.
- **Human Impact**:
    - Energy loss means more primary production required for meat-based diets vs. plant-based.
    - Example: Feeding humans via corn (vegetarian) vs. corn → cattle → humans (meat-eater) requires more energy/land.

# Biogeochemical Cycles

## Water Cycle

- **Abiotic Reservoir**: Surface & atmospheric water.
- **Entry to Food Chain**: Precipitation, plant uptake.
- **Recycling**: Transpiration.
- **Return to Abiotic**: Evaporation, runoff.
- **Distribution**: 97% oceans, 2% glaciers/ice, 1% lakes/rivers/groundwater.
- **Processes**: Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, surface/groundwater flow.

## Carbon Cycle

- **Abiotic Reservoir**: Atmosphere (CO₂), fossil fuels, soils/sediments, ocean solutes, biomass.
- **Entry to Food Chain**: Photosynthesis (carbon fixation).
- **Recycling**: Respiration, decomposition.
- **Return to Abiotic**: Respiration, combustion.
- **Processes**: Photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, volcanic activity.

## Nitrogen Cycle

- **Abiotic Reservoir**: Atmosphere (N₂).
- **Entry to Food Chain**: Nitrogen fixation by soil/aquatic bacteria → NH₄⁺ or NO₃⁻.
- **Recycling**: Decomposing & nitrifying bacteria.
- **Return to Abiotic**: Denitrifying bacteria.
- **Key Processes**:
    - **Nitrogen Fixation**: N₂ → NH₄⁺/NO₃⁻.
    - **Ammonification**: Organic N → NH₄⁺.
    - **Nitrification**: NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻.
    - **Denitrification**: NO₃⁻ → N₂.

## Phosphorus Cycle

- **Abiotic Reservoir**: Rocks, minerals, soil.
- **Entry to Food Chain**: Erosion releases soluble phosphate (PO₄³⁻); plant uptake.
- **Recycling**: Decomposing bacteria & fungi.
- **Return to Abiotic**: Loss to ocean sediment.
- **Key Features**:
    - Phosphorus is a major component of nucleic acids, phospholipids, ATP.
    - Phosphate binds tightly to soil → localized cycling.
    - Largest reservoirs: Sedimentary rocks, oceans, organisms.
