Interspecific Interactions

  • Definition: Interactions between individuals of different species.
  • Impact: Affect survival & reproduction → influence population growth rate (fitness).
  • Five Categories:
Type of Interaction Effect on Species 1 Effect on Species 2
Competition – –
Consumer‑Resource (Predation, Herbivory, Parasitism) + –
Mutualism + +
Commensalism + 0
Amensalism – 0

1. Competition (–/–)

  • Interspecific Competition: Occurs when species share limiting resources.
  • Competitive Exclusion Principle: Two species competing for the same limiting resource cannot coexist long‑term; one will outcompete the other, leading to extinction or niche shift.
  • Niche vs. Habitat:
    • Habitat: Where a species lives (its “address”).
    • Niche: Role of a species in the ecosystem (its “job”).
  • Realized Niche: Narrower niche a species actually occupies due to competition.
  • Resource Partitioning: Differentiation of ecological niches allowing similar species to coexist (e.g., lizards perching in sun vs. shade).

2. Consumer‑Resource Interactions (+/–)

  • Predation: Predator kills & eats prey.
    • Predator Adaptations: Claws, teeth, fangs, stingers, poison.
    • Prey Defenses:
      • Cryptic Coloration: Camouflage.
      • Aposematic Coloration: Bright warning colors (indicates toxicity).
      • Mimicry:
        • Batesian: Harmless species mimics harmful one.
        • Müllerian: Two unpalatable species mimic each other.
  • Herbivory: Consumer eats plants.
  • Parasitism: Parasite benefits at host’s expense.

3. Mutualism (+/+)

  • Both species benefit.
  • Examples:
    • Leaf‑cutter ants & cultivated fungi.
    • Plants & pollinators/seed‑dispersers.
    • Humans & gut bacteria.
    • Mycorrhizae (fungus‑plant roots) in nutrient‑poor soils.

4. Commensalism (+/0)

  • One species benefits, other unaffected.
  • Example: Brown‑headed cowbirds following cattle to catch flushed insects.

5. Amensalism (–/0)

  • One species harmed, other unaffected.
  • Example: Elephants crushing plants/insects while moving.

Invasive Species

  • Introduced species lacking natural enemies; reproduce/spread rapidly.
  • Negative Effects:
    • Crowd out native plants.
    • Cause native species extinction.
    • Alter native species interactions.

Evolutionary Outcomes of Interactions

  • Intraspecific Competition: Leads to traits that improve resource acquisition (e.g., Galápagos finch beak size).
  • Interspecific Competition: Can drive resource partitioning via natural selection.
  • Consumer‑Resource “Arms Race”: Prey evolve better defenses; predators evolve better offenses.
  • Plant‑Herbivore Coevolution: Plants produce toxins; herbivores evolve detoxification.
  • Mutualism: Based on resource/service exchange, not altruism; fitness benefits vary with environment.

Trophic Structure

  • Food Chain: Linear energy transfer: producers → consumers → decomposers.
  • Food Web: Interconnected food chains; species may occupy multiple trophic levels.
  • Trophic Levels:
    • Primary producers (plants, phytoplankton).
    • Primary consumers (herbivores).
    • Secondary consumers (carnivores).
    • Tertiary/quaternary consumers (top carnivores).

Limits on Food Chain Length

  1. Energy Transfer Inefficiency: ~10% energy passed between trophic levels.
  2. Biological Magnification: Toxins accumulate at higher trophic levels, reducing diversity.

Keystone Species

  • Species with disproportionately large impact on community structure.
  • Types:
    • Top Predators (e.g., sea otter, sea star Pisaster).
    • Common Prey (e.g., Antarctic krill).
    • Ecosystem Engineers (e.g., beaver).
  • Effect: Increase species diversity by controlling competitors/predators.

Community Control Models

  • Bottom‑Up Model: Lower trophic levels (nutrients, producers) control community structure.
  • Top‑Down Model (Trophic Cascade): Higher trophic levels (predators) control herbivores, which control producers.

Disturbance & Succession

  • Disturbance: Sudden environmental change (natural: fire, volcano; anthropogenic: logging, pollution).
  • Ecological Succession: Predictable species replacement over time.
    • Primary Succession: Begins on lifeless, soil‑less surfaces (e.g., volcanic rock, glacial moraine).
      • Pioneer Species: Lichens, mosses → grasses → shrubs → trees.
    • Secondary Succession: Soil intact after disturbance (e.g., post‑fire).
  • Climax Community: Stable, self‑perpetuating final stage; species composition depends on abiotic factors (climate, soil).

Why Communities Change

  1. Colonization Ability: Early species are better dispersers (e.g., dung beetles, wind/sea‑dispersed seeds).
  2. Environmental Changes Over Time: Conditions shift (e.g., light, moisture) favoring different species.

Community Function

  • Primary Productivity:
    • Gross Primary Productivity (GPP): Total energy converted by producers.
    • Net Primary Productivity (NPP): Energy stored in producer biomass available to consumers.
    • Equation: NPP = GPP – Respiration (R).
  • Ecological Efficiency: ~10% energy transferred between trophic levels.
  • Diversity‑Productivity Relationship: Higher species diversity → greater & more stable NPP.
