Endocrine System

Endocrine System Overview

  • Endocrine glands secrete hormones directly into bloodstream or surrounding tissue (no ducts).
  • Target cells have specific receptors for hormones.
  • Hormones act as long-distance chemical messengers via circulatory system.

Hormone Types & Mechanisms

  • Chemical Classes:
    • Steroid hormones (lipid/cholesterol-based): e.g., estrogen, testosterone.
      • Receptors are intracellular → hormone enters cell, binds receptor, affects gene expression.
    • Protein/peptide hormones: e.g., insulin, growth hormone.
      • Receptors on cell surface → binding triggers second messenger (e.g., cAMP) → cellular response.
    • Amino acid derivatives: e.g., thyroxine, epinephrine (from tyrosine).
  • Local regulators: act over short distances via diffusion.
    • Paracrine: act on nearby cells (e.g., cytokines, nitric oxide, prostaglandins).
    • Autocrine: act on secreting cell itself.
  • Pheromones: chemical signals between individuals of same species (e.g., trail marking, mating attraction).

Feedback Regulation

  • Negative feedback: reduces initial stimulus to maintain homeostasis.
    • E.g., insulin lowers blood glucose; high glucose stimulates insulin release, then glucose drop inhibits further release.
    • Often involves antagonistic hormones (opposing actions):
      • Glucagon (↑ glucose) vs. insulin (↓ glucose).
      • Parathyroid hormone (↑ blood Ca²⁺) vs. calcitonin (↓ blood Ca²⁺).
  • Positive feedback: amplifies initial stimulus (less common).
    • E.g., oxytocin during childbirth.

Major Endocrine Glands & Hormones

Hypothalamus & Pituitary Gland

  • Hypothalamus: master regulator; links nervous and endocrine systems.
  • Pituitary gland: two lobes.
    • Anterior pituitary:
      • Controlled by hypothalamic releasing/inhibiting hormones via portal system.
      • Secretes:
        • Growth hormone (GH): stimulates growth, metabolism; excess → gigantism, deficiency → dwarfism.
        • Prolactin (PRL): milk production.
        • Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH): gamete production.
        • Luteinizing hormone (LH): gonadal hormone release.
        • Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH): stimulates thyroid.
        • Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH): stimulates adrenal cortex.
        • Melanocyte-stimulating hormone (MSH): melanin production.
    • Posterior pituitary:
      • Stores/releases hormones made in hypothalamus:
        • Antidiuretic hormone (ADH / vasopressin): increases water reabsorption in kidneys (↓ urine volume).
        • Oxytocin: uterine contractions, milk ejection.

Thyroid Gland

  • Triiodothyronine (T3) & Thyroxine (T4):
    • Regulate metabolism, body temperature.
    • Controlled by TRH (hypothalamus) → TSH (anterior pituitary) → T3/T4 release.
  • Calcitonin: lowers blood Ca²⁺ levels (deposits Ca²⁺ in bone, increases kidney excretion).
  • Disorders:
    • Goiter: enlarged thyroid (iodine deficiency, autoimmune, etc.).
    • Hyperthyroidism (Graves’ disease): high metabolism, weight loss, rapid heartbeat, exophthalmos.
    • Hypothyroidism: low metabolism, fatigue, cold intolerance, poor memory.

Parathyroid Glands

  • Parathyroid hormone (PTH): raises blood Ca²⁺ levels.
    • Stimulates bone release of Ca²⁺, kidney reabsorption of Ca²⁺, and activates vitamin D (↑ intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption).

Pancreas (Endocrine Function)

  • Islets of Langerhans:
    • Alpha cells: secrete glucagon (↑ blood glucose).
    • Beta cells: secrete insulin (↓ blood glucose).
    • Delta cells: secrete somatostatin (inhibits insulin/glucagon release).
    • Gamma cells: secrete pancreatic polypeptide (regulates pancreatic secretion).
  • Glucose homeostasis:
    • High blood glucose → insulin release → cellular glucose uptake, glycogen storage.
    • Low blood glucose → glucagon release → glycogen breakdown, gluconeogenesis.
  • Diabetes:
    • Type I: autoimmune destruction of beta cells → insulin deficiency (treat with insulin).
    • Type II: insulin resistance (cells don’t respond to insulin); linked to diet/lifestyle.

Adrenal Glands

  • Adrenal medulla:
    • Secretes epinephrine and norepinephrine (catecholamines).
    • Part of sympathetic nervous system; mediate short-term stress response ("fight-or-flight"):
      • ↑ heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, blood glucose, alertness.
      • ↓ digestion, excretion, reproduction.
  • Adrenal cortex:
    • Secretes steroid hormones:
      • Mineralocorticoids (e.g., aldosterone): regulate salt/water balance (↑ Na⁺ reabsorption, ↑ blood volume/pressure).
      • Glucocorticoids (e.g., cortisol): ↑ blood glucose, suppress immune system, mediate long-term stress response.
      • Androgens (sex hormones).
  • Stress response:
    • Short-term: neural activation of adrenal medulla → epinephrine/norepinephrine.
    • Long-term: hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis:
      • Hypothalamus (CRH) → anterior pituitary (ACTH) → adrenal cortex (cortisol).

Pineal Gland

  • Secretes melatonin.
  • Regulates circadian rhythms (sleep-wake cycles).
  • Secretion influenced by light/dark cycles.

Gonads

  • Testes: secrete androgens (e.g., testosterone) → sperm production, male secondary sex characteristics.
  • Ovaries: secrete estrogens and progestins (e.g., progesterone) → uterine lining growth, female secondary sex characteristics, menstrual cycle.

Hormone Cascade Pathways

  • Sequence: hypothalamus → anterior pituitary → target endocrine gland → final hormone → target tissue.
  • Usually regulated by negative feedback.
  • E.g., Thyroid axis: Hypothalamus (TRH) → Anterior pituitary (TSH) → Thyroid (T3/T4) → ↑ metabolism.

Key Regulatory Systems

  • Water balance: ADH regulates kidney water reabsorption; released when blood osmolarity increases.
  • Calcium homeostasis: PTH (↑ Ca²⁺) vs. calcitonin (↓ Ca²⁺).
  • Stress response: Coordinated by adrenal glands (medulla for short-term, cortex for long-term).

Integration with Nervous System

  • Nervous system often initiates endocrine responses.
  • E.g., hypothalamus receives neural input, secretes releasing hormones to control pituitary.
  • E.g., sympathetic nerves stimulate adrenal medulla.
